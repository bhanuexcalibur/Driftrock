01The Audit

The JD asks for an audit in the first 30 days. Here’s the version I can already start from the outside.

I don’t have Driftrock’s internal deck, so I audited what every prospect and new rep sees first: the public site and published case studies. Smaller dataset than the real audit, but the gaps it surfaces usually hold up once you’re inside the building too.

driftrock.com · current state

The top-line numbers are strong and load-bearing: $1.8B+ in vehicle sales enabled, 2.4M+ leads processed annually across 24 markets for 35+ brands. The four-stage story, capture, validate, convert, measure, is clear and consistent site-wide. The case study library is unusually specific for B2B SaaS: named brands, metrics, people. Most PMMs have to fight to get that built from scratch.

gap, inferred from the outside

The proof is scattered by format, not by the sentence a rep actually hears in the room:

02Feature to Deck

Driftrock Convert closes the loop. The site says so in a bullet list. A rep needs it in one slide.

Driftrock Convert is described as an AI-powered qualification and conversion engine, with a named capability, the KMI Conversion Agent, that keeps future-model interest warm until a buyer’s ready. Here’s the gap between how that reads today and the one-pager I’d hand a rep before their next demo.

driftrock.com/products · feature framing

Convert is introduced as automated qualification journeys over WhatsApp, SMS/RCS, and email, with lapsed lead recovery and a KMI Conversion Agent for future-intent prospects. Supporting stats sit in a separate bullet: a 15 to 30% average lift in blended lead-to-sale conversion, and a modelled 46 additional vehicle sales a month at 5,000 leads. The mechanism and the proof are both there. They’re just never in the same sentence.

04Objection Handling

The four sentences I’d expect a rep to hear most, and what I’d hand them for each.

Click one open. First drafts, meant to be argued with in a real enablement session, not delivered as scripture.

Objection “We already have a CRM. Why do we need another platform?”
Response angle

Agree first: keep the CRM, this isn’t a replacement pitch. Then reframe the real question, what’s arriving in that CRM today. If roughly 1 in 10 leads industry-wide are invalid or duplicate before validation, that’s 1 in 10 records wasting sales time. Driftrock decides what reaches the CRM. The CRM still runs the deal after that.

Objection “We already run HubSpot for lead capture and nurture.”
Response angle

Ask what happens when a lead arrives without a valid vehicle model tag, or needs routing to the nearest franchised dealer by postcode. Automotive-specific problems a horizontal tool was never built for, and retrofitting them usually breaks the moment a model lineup changes. Driftrock ships that logic natively.

Objection “This feels like a lot to implement before we see results.”
Response angle

Lead with the onboarding claim directly: most brands are live on capture within two weeks, no rip-and-replace of the existing stack. Then hand over a timeline proof point rather than a promise, OMODA & JAECOO generated 15,000+ leads within 90 days of a brand-new UK launch, filtering 2,000+ invalid leads automatically along the way.

Objection “We run in 15 markets. Won’t this just add another layer of complexity?”
Response angle

Flip it: the fragmented stack per market is the complexity that already exists. BYD EU consolidated onto Driftrock across 15 markets as a single source of truth, with local publisher partners and one cross-market reporting view rather than fifteen disconnected ones. More markets is the case for one platform, not against it.
